[Ref 2]. LIGO noise characterization paper: There was a strong 500kA lightning in Burkina Faso, It happened around the same time as GW signal and could produce coincident EM signal at both sites. (Hanford and Livingston) Lightning has low frequency components as well in the 50-300Hz region of interest and this could have been picked up. Page 24, Sec 6.1 of this LIGO document talks about lightning at Burkina Faso: Click here for paper

The statement in above paper in Page 24 says "Fluxgate magnetometers indicate that magnetic disturbances at the LIGO detectors produced by coincident lightning strikes were at least 3 orders of magnitude too small to account for the amplitude of GW150914." This is based on the 50 times drop in magnetic coupling from 60Hz to 180Hz observed during calibration Click here for plot . This calibration is not applicable for external EM signals like 60*n Hz power line signals and lightning signals, which have flat coupling from 60 Hz to 180 Hz, as explained in [Ref 3] (b).

An external EM signal like lightning also can strike a power line and travel through electrical wiring inside a building. Such a signal also will have flat coupling from 60Hz-180Hz and perhaps beyond and arrive at the analog board with ADC.

60*n Hz EM interference is more likely picked up by the magnets in mirror suspension. Which means 60*n Hz EM signals travel by air/vacuum from power lines or power outlets to the magnets where they are picked up and have a flat coupling from 60Hz-180Hz.

This means, external EM signals like lightning also can be picked up by the magnets in mirror suspension and have flat coupling. If these external EM signals have an amplitude/power comparable to 60*n Hz EM tones, magnetometers will miss it.

H1 signal has amplitude/power comparable to the amplitude of 60*n Hz EM tones during null event periods and cannot be distinguished from an EM signal like lightning. This point is demonstrated with Python script H1_L1_demo_1.py: Click here for program
